## Overview

Ittan-momen (一反木綿) is a yokai originating in the folklore of Kōyama, Kimotsuki District, Kagoshima Prefecture (now Kimotsuki), in southern Kyushu, Japan. The name translates as 'one bolt of cotton' or 'one tan of cotton cloth,' where tan (反) is a traditional measurement unit for cloth approximately 10.6 meters long and 30 centimeters wide. The creature appears as a featureless long strip of plain white cotton cloth of approximately this length — no visible eyes, mouth, or appendages in traditional accounts. Despite this mundane appearance, the ittan-momen is genuinely lethal: it flies silently through the night sky and attacks humans by wrapping around their heads and necks, suffocating or strangling them to death. In some accounts the cloth spins rapidly as it attacks, wrapping victims and carrying them into the sky. The yokai exemplifies the Japanese folklore motif of the tsukumogami — objects that acquire supernatural life and often malevolent agency after 100 years of existence. Documentation of the ittan-momen appears in the Ōsumi Kimotsukigun Hōgen Shū, jointly authored by educator Nomura Denshi and folklorist Kunio Yanagita. Historians of the tradition suggest that the legend may have originated in two mundane sources: parents inventing frightening stories to discourage children from playing outside at night while adults performed farmwork, and the sight of cotton mourning flags blown by wind during funerals. Modern depictions, popularized through Mizuki Shigeru's manga series GeGeGe no Kitarō, show the ittan-momen with two eyes and arms — artistic additions not present in traditional accounts. The creature ranked first in Sakaiminato's 'First Yōkai Popularity Poll.'

## Theories & Explanations

### Paranormal / Cryptozoological

**Tsukumogami (animated object spirit)** _(Traditional Japanese folklore belief)_

Within Japanese folk belief, the ittan-momen is a tsukumogami — a piece of cloth that has accumulated spiritual energy over time and achieved supernatural animation, with inherent malevolence toward humans.

### Skeptical

**Wind-blown mourning cloth misidentification** _(Wikipedia / Ittan-momen article)_

The ittan-momen legend may originate in the frightening sight of long white cotton mourning flags blown in the wind during nighttime funerals, providing a visual stimulus that was transformed into supernatural narrative.

**Parental warning narrative** _(Wikipedia / Ittan-momen article)_

Parents in Kagoshima invented the ittan-momen story to frighten children into staying indoors at night while adults performed farmwork, providing a functional social purpose for the legend.
